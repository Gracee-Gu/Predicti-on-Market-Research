The mechanism

How a price becomes “public opinion”

A number goes through four stops on the way to a reader. The third one — the evidence that would let anyone check the first two — is the one that tends not to survive the trip.

01 · PLATFORM

Owns the market and the microphone. Publishes its own writeups of its own contracts.

→

02 · FRAMING

"Odds," "traders believe," horse-race language. Present in 85% of platform articles.

→

03 · DISCLOSURE

liquidity · spread · volume · who is actually trading

2.6% recovered
→

04 · READER

Sees a clean percentage. Has no practical way to check what stands behind it.

Independently re-querying Kalshi's own trade and market data for every contract cited in this sample recovers full liquidity evidence for 3 of 116 pairs. The rest arrive at step four with the framing intact and the disclosure gone.

The mismatch

Outside the platform

Nobody else calls it that

How often does each kind of source describe Kalshi's markets in the same "price = what people believe" register? A small, disclosed, hand-coded pass over 79 non-platform documents (CNN, AP, CFTC, congressional offices) suggests this framing is close to exclusive to the platform's own writing.

Regulators and Congress discuss Kalshi almost entirely through jurisdiction and enforcement (77% of tagged documents). Independent wire coverage leans on scandal and insider-trading stories (48%). Only the platform routinely tells you what a price means.

Method, plainly

The seven framing measures above come from a disclosed, deterministic keyword classifier applied to the platform's own text — not from trained human coders, and this page does not claim otherwise. The market-quality figures come from independently re-querying Kalshi's live trade, market, and candlestick endpoints, not from anything the platform reported. Both limits, and the full classifier rules, are documented in the paper.

Framing Without Disclosure: Representational Overreach in a Prediction-Market Platform's Own Public Communications. Yingzi Gu, Independent Research, Cornell University. Faculty Advisor: Professor Tommaso Bondi.
