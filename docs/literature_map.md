# Literature Map and Verified Research Gap

## Review protocol

This is a structured scoping review rather than a PRISMA systematic review. Sources were selected to establish the conceptual and methodological foundations needed for the empirical design. Priority was given to peer-reviewed articles, working papers from recognized research institutions, official journal pages, and primary platform/regulatory documentation. Contemporary journalism and ethics commentary are treated as evidence of the phenomenon, not as substitutes for academic theory.

Search domains:

1. prediction-market information aggregation and probability interpretation;
2. manipulation, participation, and market design;
3. market quality and microstructure;
4. media framing and institutional authority;
5. online platforms, ratings, and social learning;
6. public opinion and digital-trace measurement;
7. current media–prediction-market integration.

## Stream 1: Prediction markets as information-aggregation mechanisms

Wolfers and Zitzewitz (2004) synthesize the classical case for prediction markets: contracts can aggregate dispersed information, prices are continuously updated, and market forecasts can compare favorably with alternative forecasting mechanisms. This literature supplies the informational benchmark but does not justify every public interpretation of a displayed percentage.

Manski (2006) shows that equilibrium prices need not identify traders' mean beliefs without assumptions on preferences, wealth, and belief distributions. Wolfers and Zitzewitz (2006) derive conditions under which prices can approximate mean beliefs, while also demonstrating why the probability interpretation is conditional rather than automatic.

Berg, Nelson, and Rietz (2008) provide long-run evidence from election markets, while Page and Clemen (2013) examine calibration. Together, these works support a measured conclusion: prediction markets can be informative, but information content and probabilistic interpretation are empirical and institutional questions.

**Implication for this project:** the correct null is not “prediction markets are gambling and therefore meaningless.” A stronger study asks whether public claims exceed what the market design and quality support.

## Stream 2: Markets, surveys, and the meaning of collective belief

Dana, Dawes, and Peterson (2019) compare market forecasts with aggregated judgments obtained by simply asking forecasters. Their results challenge the assumption that a traded price is always a uniquely superior representation of collective belief.

Public-opinion scholarship treats representativeness as a sampling and measurement problem. A prediction-market price is generated by self-selected traders with heterogeneous wealth, incentives, information, and risk preferences. It therefore differs conceptually from:
- population opinion;
- survey-based expectations;
- expressed attitude;
- attention or search interest;
- social-media sentiment.

Digital-trace research also warns that online behavioral measures are not interchangeable with population attitudes. Search volume measures attention; headline tone measures media content; social-media text measures a selected population's expression. None independently validates the claim that a market price measures “public sentiment.”

**Implication:** the study operationalizes public-representation claims in media text but does not use Google Trends or social-media sentiment as a gold-standard measure of public opinion.

## Stream 3: Manipulation, heterogeneous traders, and market design

Hanson, Oprea, and Porter (2006) find in an experimental setting that attempted manipulation does not necessarily reduce accuracy because informed traders may trade against manipulators. Deck, Lin, and Porter (2013), however, show that manipulators can affect policy choices in conditional settings. Jian and Sami (2012) demonstrate that information aggregation and manipulation depend on market structure.

The broader lesson is not that markets are either manipulable or manipulation-proof. The informational effect depends on trader composition, incentives, mechanism, observability, and the response of other traders.

**Implication:** manipulation is coded as a limitation disclosed to audiences, but the study does not presume that any mention of manipulation invalidates a market.

## Stream 4: Market quality and microstructure

Market microstructure research treats liquidity as multidimensional. Bid–ask spreads capture immediacy costs; depth captures available quantity; volume and trade frequency capture activity; price impact and volatility capture how orders move prices. O'Hara (1995), Hasbrouck (2007), and Foucault, Pagano, and Röell (2013) provide the conceptual basis.

Prediction markets require additional care:
- binary prices are bounded;
- settlement creates terminal discontinuity;
- liquidity changes with time to resolution;
- thin trading can leave stale prices;
- cumulative volume can be large even when the current order book is weak;
- multi-outcome markets can violate simple comparability;
- displayed percentages may be last trade, midpoint, or platform-defined price.

Recent prediction-market work on liquidity provision reinforces that informative prices require functioning trading and market-making conditions. However, the present project does not claim to estimate a structural model of price discovery.

**Implication:** market quality must be represented as a vector and normalized within comparable platform/category/time strata. Volume alone is insufficient.

## Stream 5: Media framing and epistemic authority

Entman (1993) defines framing as selection and salience that promotes particular problem definitions, causal interpretations, evaluations, or remedies. Applied here, the key issue is not simply the presence of words such as “probability” or “bet.” It is how a text defines what the number is, whose belief it represents, what limitations matter, and what action is appropriate.

News-trust research distinguishes trust in media institutions from trust in information communicated by media. When a recognized news organization embeds a market percentage in its reporting, institutional presentation can function as an authority cue even without an explicit endorsement.

The project uses “epistemic authority” narrowly: textual claims or presentation features that position a market as a knowledge-producing device. It does not infer that audiences trusted the claim unless a separate audience study is conducted.

**Implication:** content analysis identifies legitimacy-supporting cues, not realized legitimacy effects.

## Stream 6: Platform information, ratings, and social learning

Bondi's research provides a relevant intellectual bridge. His work on consumer reviews and ratings studies how platform-generated aggregates can guide product discovery while also producing social mislearning or distortions through selection and platform design. The central parallel is that an aggregate signal can appear transparent while its interpretation depends on who contributes, how observations are selected, and how the platform displays the result.

This project deliberately does not reproduce Bondi's review-rating models. It transfers the general information-design question to a different institutional object: prediction-market prices circulated through media.

**Implication:** the contribution is adjacent to quantitative marketing and platform information design while relying on NLP, media data, and market microstructure rather than consumer-review demand estimation.

## Stream 7: Contemporary prediction markets in news

Recent media partnerships and public commentary indicate that prediction-market percentages are entering mainstream news as data products. Contemporary criticism raises concerns about commercial relationships, conflicts of interest, participant representativeness, thin markets, insider information, and gambling-like design.

These materials establish timeliness and help construct the sampling frame. They do not by themselves establish the direction or magnitude of media effects.

## Verified gap

The literature contains:
- theories and tests of prediction-market accuracy;
- conditions for interpreting prices as probabilities;
- research on manipulation and mechanism design;
- mature market-quality concepts;
- media-framing theory;
- platform-information and social-learning theory.

It does **not yet provide a well-established empirical design that jointly measures**:

1. how media and platforms broaden the meaning of a prediction-market price;
2. whether market-quality limitations are disclosed at the point of public representation;
3. whether the strength of authority and public-representation claims is aligned with the quality of the cited contract; and
4. where high-claim, low-quality, low-disclosure configurations occur.

This intersection is the project's defensible contribution. The novelty claim is intentionally local: the project does not claim to invent the study of prediction markets, media framing, or market quality separately.

## What is mature and should not be duplicated

- generic tests of whether prediction markets outperform polls;
- a two-event comparison of market price with Google Trends;
- generic sentiment analysis of prediction-market articles;
- simple keyword counts without validated constructs;
- treating volume as the sole measure of information quality;
- causal claims about audience trust from observational text.

## What is feasible and valuable here

- a reproducible, stratified corpus of media/platform/institutional texts;
- validated sentence-level framing measures;
- contract-level market-quality extraction;
- market-mention linkage;
- transparent overreach classification;
- robustness and case audits.
